Abstract

Malaysia's graduate labour market faces critical challenges, with 48.6% of graduates overqualified for their positions and starting salaries approaching minimum wage levels at RM1,500 monthly, creating significant wage transparency gaps. Existing solutions such as general salary platforms (JobStreet, Glassdoor), university career counseling, and cost-of-living calculators (Numbeo) provide fragmented support but suffer from critical gaps: lack of Malaysia-specific district-level data, limited multilingual accessibility, passive information delivery without personalized guidance, and absence of AI-driven predictive analytics or practical negotiation training. WageWise addresses these gaps as a comprehensive mobile application that integrates data-driven salary insights, AI-powered labour law consultation, and personalized wage negotiation training. The system encompasses salary data analytics modules utilizing machine learning models for predictive insights, intelligent district-and-profession-adjusted cost-of-living calculators, and AI-powered chatbot consultation services. The application is developed using Flutter framework with ARB translation support for multilingual accessibility across Bahasa Malaysia, English, Mandarin, and Tamil, supported by Supabase backend infrastructure. Testing encompasses functional validation of all modules, accuracy assessment of ML-based salary predictions, multilingual interface verification, and user experience evaluation through usability testing with fresh graduates. The expected outcomes include a 30% improvement in users' salary negotiation confidence and measurable increases in starting salaries for fresh graduates, directly contributing to SDG 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth) and SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities) by empowering graduates with data-driven negotiation capabilities and addressing wage transparency gaps in Malaysia's labour market.

# Introduction

Malaysia's graduate labour market faces a critical wage transparency crisis, with 48.6% of graduates overqualified for their positions ([Khazanah Research Institute, 2024](https://www.krinstitute.org/publications/shifting-tides-charting-career-progression-of-malaysias-skilled-talents)) and starting salaries for many fresh graduates stagnating near the RM1,700 minimum wage floor (Minimum Wages Order, 2024), leaving fresh graduates vulnerable to exploitation due to lack of reliable salary benchmarks and employment rights knowledge. Existing solutions such as JobStreet, Glassdoor, university career services, and cost-of-living calculators provide fragmented support but lack Malaysia-specific district-level data, multilingual accessibility, personalized guidance, and integrated AI-driven analytics. WageWise addresses these gaps as a comprehensive Android mobile application developed using Flutter framework and Supabase backend, integrating three core modules: a Salary Intelligence Platform utilizing machine learning analysis of the Hugging Face Malaysian job dataset for personalized compensation benchmarks, an AI-Powered Labour Rights Chatbot trained on Malaysian employment legislation for accessible legal guidance and contract analysis, and a Cost-of-Living Wage Evaluator offering district-level financial comparisons with statutory deduction calculations. Supporting Bahasa Malaysia, English, Mandarin, and Tamil, WageWise aims to achieve a 30% improvement in salary negotiation confidence and measurable increases in starting salaries for fresh graduates, empowering Malaysia's graduates with data-driven tools to secure fair compensation and establish sustainable career foundations.

## Overview/ Background of the study

### Brief description of the domain

The graduate employment landscape in Malaysia represents a critical intersection of education, economics, and career development. Fresh graduates entering the workforce face significant challenges in understanding their market value and negotiating fair compensation packages. This domain encompasses salary transparency, employment rights awareness, cost-of-living considerations, and wage negotiation skills—all essential components for establishing sustainable career foundations and economic stability.

### Current situation or trends related to the problem

Malaysia's graduate labour market is experiencing alarming trends that undermine the value of higher education. Current statistics reveal that 48.6% of graduates are overqualified for their positions, while starting salaries have stagnated near minimum wage levels at approximately RM1,500 monthly ([Khazanah Research Institute, 2024](https://www.krinstitute.org/publications/shifting-tides-charting-career-progression-of-malaysias-skilled-talents)). This wage compression creates substantial transparency gaps, leaving graduates without reliable benchmarks for fair compensation. The situation is further complicated by regional cost-of-living variations across Malaysia's districts, currency fluctuations affecting purchasing power, and the absence of personalized guidance during critical salary negotiation phases. Recent graduates often accept undervalued offers due to information asymmetry and lack of negotiation confidence, perpetuating cycles of wage inequity.

### Why this problem is significant

This problem holds profound implications for both individual graduates and Malaysia's broader economic development. At the individual level, accepting undervalued initial salaries creates long-term earning deficits, as future raises typically build upon starting compensation. This wage suppression delays financial independence, affects quality of life, and diminishes returns on educational investments. At the national level, persistent graduate underemployment and wage inequity directly contradict Malaysia's aspirations toward high-income nation status and conflict with Sustainable Development Goals, particularly SDG 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth) and SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities). The lack of wage transparency also enables discriminatory practices and perpetuates economic disparities across demographic groups and geographic regions. Furthermore, graduates without proper negotiation skills and market awareness remain vulnerable to exploitation, undermining labor market efficiency and social mobility.

### Gap or limitation in existing solution

Current solutions addressing graduate salary concerns suffer from critical limitations that prevent effective problem resolution. General salary platforms like JobStreet and Glassdoor provide aggregated data but lack Malaysia-specific granularity at the district level, failing to account for regional cost-of-living variations and local market conditions. University career counseling services, while valuable, operate with limited resources and cannot provide personalized, data-driven salary insights to every graduate. Cost-of-living calculators such as Numbeo offer generic comparisons but don't integrate profession-specific salary data or provide actionable negotiation guidance. Critically, no existing solution combines AI-powered predictive analytics, labor law consultation, multilingual accessibility (Bahasa Malaysia, English, Mandarin, Tamil), and practical negotiation training in a single comprehensive platform. The fragmented nature of current tools forces graduates to piece together information from multiple sources without personalized guidance or confidence-building support.

### Introducing proposed project

WageWise is proposed as a comprehensive mobile application designed to bridge these critical gaps by empowering Malaysian fresh graduates with data-driven salary negotiation capabilities. The application integrates machine learning-powered salary insights that provide Malaysia-specific, district-level compensation benchmarks adjusted for profession, education level, and regional factors. An AI-powered chatbot offers instant labor law consultation and personalized negotiation advice in multiple languages, ensuring accessibility across Malaysia's diverse linguistic landscape. The platform features intelligent cost-of-living calculators that dynamically adjust salary expectations based on location-specific expenses, and interactive wage negotiation training modules that build practical skills through scenario-based learning. Developed using Flutter framework with Supabase backend infrastructure, WageWise delivers a seamless, multilingual user experience while maintaining data accuracy and security. By consolidating fragmented information sources into a single, intelligent platform, WageWise aims to achieve a 30% improvement in users' salary negotiation confidence and measurable increases in starting salaries for fresh graduates, directly contributing to wage transparency and economic equity in Malaysia's graduate labour market.

## Problem Statements

### Problem 1: Lack of Transparent Salary Information and Centralized Market Data

Malaysia's labour market continues to struggle with severe information asymmetry, disproportionately affecting fresh graduates who lack access to verified market data. Policymakers have flagged the absence of transparent salary disclosures as a systemic failure, with proposals for mandatory salary ranges in job advertisements gaining traction to curb wage suppression (HRM Asia, 2023). This opacity has contributed to persistent underemployment; in 2024, 32.2% of employed graduates—approximately 1.60 million individuals—were trapped in skill-related underemployment, working in semi-skilled or low-skilled roles that did not require tertiary qualifications (Department of Statistics Malaysia [DOSM], 2025). While this figure positions Malaysia favorably compared to regional peers like Indonesia (42.2%) and the Philippines (56.7%), it lags significantly behind advanced economies such as the United States (27.3%) and Australia (27.5%) (DOSM, 2025). The situation has deteriorated further in late 2025; Q3 data revealed that skill-related underemployment climbed to 1.96 million persons (35.5%), while youth unemployment (aged 15–24) remained critically high at 10.1%, far exceeding the national unemployment average of 3.0% (The Edge Malaysia, 2025). Without a centralized platform to bridge this information gap, young workers remain vulnerable to undervalued offers.

### Problem 2: Limited Negotiation Skills and Labour Rights Awareness Among Fresh Graduates

Fresh graduates in Malaysia often enter the workforce with a "negotiation deficit," stemming from inadequate knowledge of their rights and market value. Research highlights that alongside quality of education concerns, unrealistic salary expectations—often resulting from a lack of data rather than entitlement—are primary drivers of graduate unemployment (al-Qanatir, 2024). The Malaysian Employers Federation (MEF) explicitly warns that the new RM1,700 minimum wage (effective February 2025) should not be treated as a default starting salary for degree holders, arguing that compensation must reflect job complexity and productivity (Free Malaysia Today, 2025). However, a critical regulatory gap creates a trap for new entrants: the RM1,700 mandate does not apply to micro-enterprises (fewer than 5 employees) until August 1, 2025, leaving graduates joining small startups legally vulnerable to offers as low as RM1,500 (Lexology, 2025). Without practical training in contract evaluation and salary negotiation, graduates are often coerced into accepting these "baseline" wages, effectively anchoring their long-term earning potential to a statutory floor intended for unskilled labour.

### Problem 3: Inadequate Cost-of-Living Adjusted Wage Evaluation Tools

A significant disconnect exists between offered salaries and the actual cost of living across Malaysia's diverse districts, leading to "blind" acceptance of job offers that result in financial strain. The Department of Statistics Malaysia recently introduced the Basic Expenditure of Decent Living (PAKW) index, which highlights massive intra-state disparities; for instance, living costs in the urban district of Petaling are nearly double those in rural Sabak Bernam, yet standard salary offers rarely account for this granularity (DOSM, 2025). Although the median monthly salary for graduates rose to RM4,521 in 2024, top-performing states like Kuala Lumpur (RM5,888) and Putrajaya (RM5,723) skew the national average, masking the reality in lower-cost regions (DOSM, 2025). Industry reports confirm that while urban centers offer higher nominal wages, the real purchasing power often stagnates due to escalating housing and food costs (Job Majestic, 2024). Academic analysis further reveals that wage stagnation for semi-skilled graduates persists for years, partly because job seekers lack the tools to calculate their "real wage" based on location-specific inflation before accepting a position (Ting & Yong, 2025).

## Project objectives

### To Develop AI-Assisted Salary Intelligence Module For Fresh graduates and early-career professionals assess their own market value

This module helps fresh graduates and early-career professionals evaluate job offers by comparing them against verified market salary ranges. Using the Hugging Face job dataset ([https://huggingface.co/datasets/azrai99/job-dataset](https://huggingface.co/datasets/azrai99/job-dataset)), it accepts inputs such as job title, industry, education, and experience to return personalised salary benchmarks minimum, median, and maximum and classifies any offer as below, within, or above market rate within the Malaysian job market.

### To Implement AI-Based Wage Guidance & Labour Rights Chatbot For young professionals learn to negotiate for their wage and aware of their rights under Malaysian employment law 

This module provides fresh graduates with personalised negotiation tips and employment rights awareness grounded in Malaysian labour law, including the Employment Act 1955 and Industrial Relations Act 1967. Through a conversational interface, users can ask plain-language questions and upload contract clauses for analysis, with the system flagging potentially non-compliant or exploitative terms and delivering clear, jargon-free guidance on working hours, leave, EPF contributions, and termination procedures.

### To Implement Location-Based Real Wage Evaluation For Graduates evaluate compensation based on actual purchasing power rather than surface-level salary figures.

This module goes beyond nominal salary figures by calculating the true affordability of a job offer based on district-level cost-of-living data across major Malaysian cities, factoring in statutory deductions such as EPF, SOCSO, and income tax to produce a detailed monthly financial breakdown. Side-by-side comparisons clearly illustrate whether a higher nominal salary in an expensive city provides better real financial outcomes, enabling graduates to evaluate compensation based on actual purchasing power rather than surface-level salary figures.

WageWise is a mobile application developed using Flutter framework for Android platform that addresses wage transparency and employment rights challenges for Malaysian fresh graduates and final year students. The application encompasses three integrated functional modules: Salary Intelligence Platform, AI-Powered Labour Rights Chatbot, and Cost-of-Living Wage Evaluator.

## Target Audience

Fresh Graduates & Final Year Students (Age 20–26)

Fresh graduates and final year students entering the workforce face critical vulnerability to wage exploitation due to lack of market knowledge, limited negotiation experience, and information asymmetry in Malaysia's labour market. This demographic represents the core user base of WageWise, comprising three distinct subgroups with varying but interconnected needs throughout their early career journey.

Final year students are actively preparing for job hunting, attending career fairs, researching potential employers, and beginning initial salary investigations to set realistic expectations before entering the job market. WageWise supports this subgroup by providing early access to salary intelligence for their target job roles and industries, enabling them to understand market-rate compensation ranges before receiving their first offers. The platform's Cost-of-Living Wage Evaluator helps students assess geographic opportunities, comparing potential salaries across different Malaysian cities to inform decisions about where to begin their careers. The AI-Powered Labour Rights Chatbot educates pre-employment students on fundamental employment rights, statutory benefits (EPF, SOCSO, annual leave entitlements), and contract essentials, building foundational knowledge before they encounter actual employment agreements. This proactive preparation empowers final year students to enter the job market with confidence, realistic salary expectations grounded in data, and awareness of fair employment standards.

Subgroup B: Fresh Graduates (0–1 Year Experience)

Fresh graduates with zero to one year of experience are currently navigating their first job offers, evaluating employment contracts, and facing immediate salary negotiation decisions that will anchor their long-term earning potential. WageWise addresses this critical transition phase by providing personalized salary estimates based on their specific educational qualifications, chosen industry sector, and job title, enabling direct comparison between offered salaries and market benchmarks. Subgroup C: Early-Career Professionals (1–3 Years Experience)

Early-career professionals with one to three years of experience are considering job transitions, seeking salary benchmarking for performance reviews or promotions, and building negotiation skills for career advancement beyond entry-level positions. The AI-Powered Labour Rights Chatbot assists with contract comparison when considering job changes, highlighting differences in benefits packages, notice requirements, and employment terms between current and prospective positions. This subgroup leverages WageWise for ongoing career management rather than one-time job search support, utilizing the platform to track market salary evolution and maintain awareness of their market value throughout early career development.

# Literature Review

## Introduction to Literature Review

This literature review examines the technological foundations and existing systems relevant to WageWise, a comprehensive platform designed to empower Malaysian graduates and early-career professionals through wage transparency, negotiation coaching, and employment rights education. The review focuses on three primary domains: salary prediction using machine learning, AI-powered negotiation training systems, and employment rights education platforms. Additionally, it explores cost-of-living analysis tools and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) architectures that underpin the technical implementation of WageWise.

The aim of this review is to establish a comprehensive understanding of current technologies and methodologies that can be applied to create an integrated platform addressing wage inequity and information asymmetry faced by Malaysian job seekers.

## Overview of the Project Domain/ Background Concepts

### Artificial Intelligence in Career Development and Compensation Analysis

Artificial intelligence (AI) has fundamentally transformed the recruitment and compensation landscape. In the context of WageWise, the domain leverages Supervised Machine Learning (SML) to perform predictive analytics on wage data.

Unlike traditional static salary tables, AI models can process high-dimensional data—variables such as years of experience (YoE), academic qualifications (CGPA/Degree type), technical certifications, and geographic location—to identify non-linear correlations. For instance, the "wage premium" for a software engineer in Kuala Lumpur versus George Town may vary significantly based on industry saturation. WageWise utilizes these patterns to provide a "Fair Market Value" (FMV) for users, moving beyond simple averages to provide personalized salary estimations. According to Ghorai and Barik (2025), supervised learning algorithms such as Random Forest and XGBoost are particularly effective in salary prediction because they can handle complex, multi-variable datasets that traditional linear models often overlook.

### Large Language Models (LLMs) 

Large Language Models (LLMs) function through a Transformer architecture that utilizes a self-attention mechanism to process and understand the context of human language. This process begins with tokenization, where text is broken into numerical units and mapped into high-dimensional vector embeddings, allowing the model to mathematically recognize semantic relationships between words, such as "salary" and "compensation." By assigning varying weights to words based on their relevance within a sequence, the model can maintain logical coherence over long sentences, which is essential for WageWise to interpret complex user queries about employment contracts or negotiation scenarios.

At their core, LLMs operate as advanced probabilistic engines rather than factual databases. During the inference phase, the model predicts the most statistically likely next token in a sequence based on patterns identified during its extensive training on global datasets. While this allows for human-like conversational fluency, it also leads to "hallucinations" and knowledge cutoffs where the model may generate outdated or incorrect legal information. Consequently, for a specialized tool like WageWise, the LLM serves as a reasoning engine that must be grounded by a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architecture to ensure all advice is strictly aligned with current Malaysian labor laws and economic data. This grounding is supported by the research of Gao et al. (2023), who demonstrate that RAG effectively mitigates the hallucination issues of LLMs by retrieving relevant, up-to-date documents before the generation process occurs.

### Multilingual Natural Language Processing (NLP)

Given Malaysia’s diverse demographic, the project domain involves Cross-Lingual Information Retrieval (CLIR). This concept allows a user to ask a question in Bahasa Malaysia while the system retrieves the answer from a technical document originally written in English. By utilizing Flutter’s ARB translation framework and multilingual embedding models, WageWise ensures that the "information asymmetry" is bridged across linguistic barriers, making complex labor laws accessible to vernacular-speaking graduates.

## Review of existing Systems / Applications

Several platforms currently address salary prediction and compensation benchmarking in the Malaysian market. This section reviews four key platforms that represent the current landscape of salary intelligence tools available to Malaysian graduates: Glassdoor Malaysia, PAYGAP Asia, JobStreet Malaysia, and PayScale. Each platform demonstrates distinct strengths and limitations that inform the design of WageWise.

### Glassdoor Malaysia

Glassdoor operates as a global leader in company reviews and salary benchmarks, providing crowdsourced salary information alongside company reviews that enable job seekers to research employer-specific compensation ranges (Glassdoor, 2026). The platform allows users to view salary data for specific roles and companies, with particular strength in covering multinational corporations operating in Malaysia.

Strengths: The platform's main strengths include an extensive database of multinational corporation (MNC) salaries and high brand trust among job seekers globally. Technologically, the platform leverages sophisticated Machine Learning (ML) algorithms for its "Know Your Worth" tool, which calculates a user's market value by analyzing millions of data points across job titles, locations, and seniority. Users can access salary ranges alongside company reviews, workplace culture insights, and interview experiences, providing a holistic view of potential employers. Furthermore, Glassdoor utilizes Natural Language Processing (NLP) to perform sentiment analysis on millions of user reviews, allowing the system to categorize "Pros" and "Cons" into structured data. The platform's global reach ensures reasonable coverage of major corporations operating in Malaysia, particularly in urban centers like Kuala Lumpur and Penang.

Limitations: However, data for the Malaysian market is of0ten urban-centric and lacks the granularity needed for fresh graduates in semi-skilled roles, as evidenced by sparse submissions for entry-level positions outside major cities. The platform faces limitations in data verification, with self-reported information potentially introducing inaccuracies. Most critically, Glassdoor provides raw salary data but offers no negotiation training or labor law education, representing a significant gap in holistic career support. The platform does not integrate cost-of-living analysis or provide guidance on understanding Malaysian employment rights under the Employment Act 1955.

### PAYGAP Asia

PAYGAP Asia represents a regional initiative specifically focused on breaking the salary taboo through anonymous data sharing in Asian markets (PAYGAP Asia, 2024). Founded in 2022 by Prestine Davekhaw, the platform went viral within one week on Instagram and has grown to over 300,000 followers across Instagram and TikTok through man-on-the-street interviews asking people how much they earn (CNBC, 2024). The platform transitioned from purely social media content to a digital database in 2024, operating on a give-to-get model where users share their salary data to gain access to the community database.

Strengths: PAYGAP Asia demonstrates high cultural relevance and transparency specifically for the Asian market, addressing cultural taboos around salary discussions that are particularly strong in Malaysian society.A key technological strength is its rigorous data verification workflow; unlike purely anonymous forums, PAYGAP utilizes digital verification pipelines, including Optical Character Recognition (OCR) or human-in-the-loop (HITL) systems to verify salaries through payslip submissions, significantly improving data reliability. The platform offers 'warm data' that combines individual stories with salary figures rather than cold statistical averages, helping users understand the human context behind compensation levels. Salaries are verified through payslip submissions, improving data reliability compared to purely self-reported platforms. The platform's social media presence and storytelling approach have successfully engaged younger workers who might not use traditional salary research tools.

Limitations: Despite its cultural relevance, PAYGAP Asia functions primarily as a passive information list lacking AI-driven personalized guidance for career decisions. The platform does not provide district-specific cost-of-living analysis to help users understand salary adequacy across different Malaysian cities. Mobile optimization and interactive tools for career decision-making remain limited compared to dedicated career platforms. Most critically, while the platform successfully addresses the cultural taboo around salary discussions, it does not provide negotiation training or employment rights education, leaving users with data but without skills or legal knowledge to act on that information effectively.

Strengths: The platform's main strength lies in its massive user base, with the majority of Malaysian employers and job seekers using the platform for recruitment and job search activities.This market dominance is supported by a powerful AI-driven recommendation engine that uses collaborative filtering and deep learning to match candidates with roles based on their salary expectations and historical behavior. ## Review of Techniques/ Methods/ Technologies

### Machine Learning Algorithms for Salary Prediction

Random Forest Regression has emerged as a leading algorithm for salary prediction due to its ability to handle non-linear relationships and feature interactions. The algorithm constructs multiple decision trees during training and outputs the average prediction, reducing overfitting risks. Research achieved 81% accuracy (R² = 0.81) using Random Forest on Stack Overflow Developer Survey data, with Mean Absolute Error of 17,166 and Root Mean Squared Error of 22,031. (Ghorai, P., & Barik, R., 2025) 

XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) represents an advanced ensemble method that builds trees sequentially, with each tree correcting errors from previous trees. Comparative analyses show that XGBoost and Random Forest consistently outperform simpler methods like linear regression, particularly for datasets with complex feature interactions such as job titles, skills, and geographic factors. Ensemble models have achieved R² values up to 0.94 in employee salary prediction tasks. (Eichinger, F., & Mayer, M., 2022) 

Feature engineering plays a critical role in model performance, involving transformation of raw data into meaningful predictors. Relevant techniques include encoding categorical variables (job titles, industries) using methods like one-hot encoding or target encoding, creating interaction features (e.g., combining experience level with education), normalizing numerical features to prevent scale bias, and extracting skills or certifications from job descriptions using natural language processing. 

Model validation ensures that predictions generalize to new data rather than merely memorizing training examples. Standard approaches include cross-validation, holdout validation, and temporal validation for time-series data. These validation techniques make a system assess model reliability before deployment (Ghorai & Barik, 2025).

For WageWise, Random Forest is selected as the primary algorithm due to its robustness against overfitting and interpretability, making it suitable for deployment on user devices with limited computational resources. While XGBoost can achieve marginally higher accuracy, Random Forest provides optimal balance between prediction performance (R² > 0.80), training efficiency, and model stability for fresh graduate salary prediction, particularly when combined with target encoding for categorical variables and cross-validation to ensure reliable generalization to new user queries. 

### Retrieval_Augmented Generation(RAG) for LLM Applications

Naive RAG Architecture represents the foundational approach where a user query triggers document retrieval, followed by augmenting the query with retrieved content before LLM generation. The process involves indexing documents by chunking text into segments, generating embeddings using models like BERT, storing embeddings in vector databases (e.g., ChromaDB, Pinecone), and retrieving the most relevant chunks based on query similarity. 

Advanced RAG Optimization addresses limitations of naive approaches through improved retrieval quality and generation faithfulness. Techniques include query expansion, hybrid retrieval combining dense embeddings with keyword matching, reranking retrieved documents, and citation generation..

Prompt Engineering optimises LLM behaviour through careful instruction design. Effective strategies include system prompts, few-shot examples, chain-of-thought prompting, and constraint specification. 

This technique can benefit WageWise's employment rights component by enabling retrieval of specific sections from legislation such as the Employment Act 1955 or Industrial Relations Act 1967 when addressing user questions about labour rights. Hybrid retrieval approaches could be valuable for such systems as they can handle both conceptual questions and specific statutory references. For negotiation coaching features, prompt engineering techniques may be applied to define roleplay scenarios and establish feedback criteria for evaluating user responses (Gao et al., 2023). 

### Natural Language Processing for Contract Analysis

Named Entity Recognition (NER) identifies specific entities in text such as monetary amounts, dates, job titles, and legal clauses. This technique can be applied to employment contracts to extract key terms including salary figures, working hours, overtime policies, leave entitlements, and termination notice periods. 

Text Classification categorises document segments according to predefined categories. In the employment context, relevant categories include compliant clauses, potentially problematic clauses, illegal clauses, and missing protections. 

Semantic Similarity Matching compares contract clauses against known legal violations or unfair practices by computing embedding-based similarity scores. This enables the system to flag clauses semantically similar to known problematic language, even if exact wording differs. For example, a clause stating ‘Employees must work additional hours without additional compensation’ would be flagged as similar to ‘unlimited unpaid overtime’ patterns that violate Section 60A of the Employment Act .

For WageWise's contract review component, NER techniques could be employed to automatically identify clauses requiring legal scrutiny. Classification approaches may be implemented using labelled examples of employment contracts to support red-flag detection, enabling the system to assist users in understanding potential contractual issues before signing. 

### Cost-of-Living Index Construction

Basket-of-Goods Methodology establishes cost-of-living (COL) indices by tracking prices of representative goods and services in different locations. The approach involves defining consumption categories (housing, food, transportation, healthcare, utilities), selecting representative items, collecting price data from local sources, and calculating weighted averages based on typical spending patterns. 

Net Income Calculation adjusts gross salaries for mandatory deductions including EPF contributions (11% employee, 13% employer), SOCSO contributions, and income tax based on progressive rates. This provides a more accurate basis for cost-of-living comparisons than gross salary alone, as take-home pay determines actual purchasing power. 

Location Arbitrage Analysis identifies opportunities where lower-cost locations offer equivalent or superior purchasing power despite lower nominal salaries. For example, a graduate might discover that a RM 4,000 monthly salary in Penang provides greater disposable income than a RM 4,500 salary in Kuala Lumpur due to housing cost differentials. 

For WageWise, this methodology can be applied to major Malaysian cities to create location-specific expense profiles. Malaysian-specific tax and contribution calculators could be implemented to help users understand their net income under different salary scenarios. This type of analysis may serve as a valuable component of location-aware career decision support systems ([Salary.com](http://salary.com), 2025).

## Research Gap/ Identified Limitations

### Lack of Integrated Career Decision Support Systems

Existing systems address salary prediction, negotiation training, employment rights, or cost-of-living analysis in isolation, requiring users to navigate multiple disconnected platforms (as evidenced by comparative analysis in Section 2.5). This fragmentation creates inefficiencies and cognitive burden, as users must manually synthesize information from different sources to make informed career decisions. For instance, a graduate comparing job offers must separately research salary benchmarks on PayScale, practice negotiations through academic systems (if accessible), verify legal compliance on government websites, and calculate cost-of-living differences using Numbeo—each with different interfaces and data formats.

This gap directly relates to WageWise's value proposition: providing a unified platform where salary intelligence may inform negotiation preparation, employment rights knowledge may prevent acceptance of illegal terms, and cost-of-living analysis enables location-aware decisions. An integrated approach could enable workflows such as: 'View predicted salary range for this position then Practice negotiating to the 75th percentile then Verify contract compliance with Employment Act then Compare net disposable income across cities'—all within a single system.

### Insufficient Focus on Malaysian Market and Regulatory Context

Global platforms like PayScale, Glassdoor, and LinkedIn provide limited coverage of Malaysian salary data and fail to account for local regulatory frameworks such as EPF contributions, SOCSO deductions, and Employment Act provisions (as identified in Section 2.3.1). This creates information gaps for Malaysian users who cannot accurately assess their market value or understand their legal protections.

Even general AI negotiation systems lack cultural specificity regarding Malaysian workplace norms, business communication styles, and salary negotiation conventions that differ from Western contexts. For example, Malaysian negotiations may place greater emphasis on indirect communication and relationship preservation compared to direct assertiveness common in U.S. business culture (implied by cultural business literature).

WageWise can address this gap by utilizing Malaysian-specific datasets (azrai99/job-dataset), incorporating Employment Act 1955 and Industrial Relations Act 1967 as knowledge bases, implementing Malaysian tax and EPF calculators, and designing negotiation scenarios reflecting local workplace dynamics. This localization approach aims to provide relevant, actionable guidance for Malaysian users rather than generic international advice. 

### Limited Accessibility of Negotiation Training for Early-Career Individuals

Advanced negotiation training remains concentrated in expensive MBA programs, executive education courses, and professional development workshops that are financially inaccessible to recent graduates (Program on Negotiation at Harvard Law School, 2025b). While AI systems like ASU's DNA and MIT's coaching bots democratize access within academic settings, they are not available to the broader public of job seekers who need negotiation skills when entering the workforce.

This accessibility gap disproportionately affects individuals from lower socioeconomic backgrounds who most need strong negotiation skills to maximize their earnings but cannot afford training programs. The result perpetuates information asymmetry where sophisticated negotiators (often from privileged backgrounds with access to coaching) secure better compensation than equally qualified peers who lack negotiation training.

WageWise aims to address this gap by providing affordable, AI-powered negotiation coaching accessible to anyone with internet connectivity. The cloud API model leverages free-tier services from Google Gemini API (60 requests per minute at no cost), making the core features available without subscription fees for typical individual usage. The architecture eliminates hardware barriers, users do not need expensive laptops or high-end smartphones, enabling access from any internet-connected device including budget smartphones. By packaging AI-driven negotiation training with machine learning-powered salary market data, users gain both adaptive coaching skills and personalized information needed for effective salary discussions, leveling the playing field regardless of educational background, financial resources, or device ownership. 

### Absence of Automated Employment Contract Review Tools

While legal professionals offer contract review services, these are costly (often RM 500-2000 per consultation in Malaysia, based on typical legal fee structures) and inaccessible to many graduates evaluating their first job offers (implied by legal services market). Existing free resources provide general guidance on contract terms but cannot analyze specific document language to identify violations or unfair clauses tailored to individual users' situations.

This gap leaves graduates vulnerable to accepting positions with illegal provisions (e.g., unpaid overtime clauses violating Section 60A of Employment Act 1955) or unfair terms (e.g., excessive probation periods, unclear termination procedures) that they cannot recognize without legal expertise. The consequences include wage theft, overwork without compensation, and difficulty enforcing rights if disputes arise.

WageWise could help address this gap through NER and semantic similarity matching (Section 2.4.3) to automatically identify potentially problematic clauses in uploaded employment contracts. The system may compare contract language against known patterns of Employment Act violations, highlights questionable provisions with legal explanations (sourced from RAG-based retrieval of act text), and suggests specific questions users should ask employers before signing. While not replacing professional legal advice for complex situations, this tool could provide an accessible first-line defense against common exploitative practices.

### Static Data and Outdated Information in Existing Platforms

Many salary benchmarking platforms rely on annually updated survey data that may not reflect current market conditions, particularly during periods of rapid economic change such as inflation surges or industry disruptions (implied by platform review in Section 2.3.1). Similarly, static employment law guides require manual updates to incorporate legislative amendments, creating risks that users receive outdated legal information.

The gap is particularly problematic for dynamic information domains such as minimum wage rates (increased from RM 1,500 to RM 1,700 in Malaysia as of February 2025), new employment protections (e.g., expanded gig worker coverage under proposed Employment Act amendments), and evolving labor market trends (e.g., remote work normalization affecting geographic salary variations).

WageWise can partially address this through RAG architecture (Section 2.4.2) for employment rights information, enabling straightforward updates by adding new legal documents to the vector database without model retraining. For salary data, the system may support periodic retraining with updated datasets, though this represents an area for future enhancement through automated data pipeline development. The combination of updateable knowledge bases and flexible ML models provides better currency than static platform content.

WageWise adopts Random Forest and XGBoost algorithms for salary prediction based on extensive literature evidence demonstrating their superiority for this application domain. Research by Ghorai and Barik (2025) achieved 81% accuracy using Random Forest on salary prediction tasks, while Dritsas et al. Third, the Malaysian job dataset from HuggingFace (azrai99/job-dataset) contains categorical variables and feature interactions that ensemble methods are specifically designed to exploit, as documented in the techniques review (Section 2.4.1).

For the employment rights chatbot, WageWise may implement Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architecture based on findings from Gao et al. (2023) and NVIDIA (2025) demonstrating that RAG overcomes Large Language Model limitations including hallucination and knowledge cutoff issues. This directly addresses the gap identified in Section 2.6.6 where static legal guides like those from JY Ko Law require manual updates and cannot provide conversational interfaces. The RAG approach enables the system to retrieve authoritative sections from the Employment Act 1955 and Industrial Relations Act 1967 before generating responses, ensuring legal accuracy while maintaining natural language interaction.

Phase 1: Requirements Gathering and Analysis

The deliverable is a functional salary prediction component achieving target accuracy (R² > 0.80) with an intuitive user interface.

Phase 4: Component Development - AI Negotiation Coach 

Activities include implementing the RAG architecture by creating vector embeddings of negotiation knowledge base documents using ChromaDB, designing system prompts defining the AI's role as a Malaysian salary negotiation coach, and developing predefined practice scenarios including 'The Lowball Offer,' 'Asking for a Raise,' and 'Counter-Offer Negotiation.' Testing involves validating that the coach provides culturally appropriate feedback, references Malaysian workplace norms, and maintains conversation context across multi-turn interactions. The deliverable is a functional negotiation coach running on localhost with realistic roleplay scenarios and constructive feedback mechanisms.

Phase 5: Component Development - Employment Rights Chatbot and COL Evaluator 

This phase implements the employment rights chatbot by indexing legal documents in ChromaDB, implementing semantic search to retrieve relevant sections based on user queries, and designing prompts instructing the AI to cite specific statutory sections when answering questions. The contract review feature is developed using Named Entity Recognition to extract key clauses and semantic similarity matching to identify potentially illegal provisions. Simultaneously, the cost-of-living evaluator is built by implementing EPF and SOCSO calculators based on Malaysian regulations, creating cost indices for major cities (Kuala Lumpur, Penang, Johor Bahru), and developing comparison visualizations showing net disposable income across locations. Testing validates legal accuracy by cross-referencing chatbot responses against Employment Act provisions and ensures contract review correctly flags known violations. The deliverable includes a functional employment rights chatbot with contract review capabilities and a comprehensive cost-of-living comparison tool.

Phase 6: System Integration, Testing, and Documentation 

Final phase activities include integrating all components into a unified Streamlit application with navigation between modules, conducting end-to-end testing of complete user workflows (e.g., viewing salary prediction, practicing negotiation, reviewing contract, comparing locations), performing usability testing with target users to gather feedback on interface clarity and feature usefulness, optimizing performance including model inference speed and response times, and preparing deployment documentation covering installation instructions, system requirements, and troubleshooting guides. The deliverable is a fully functional WageWise system ready for user deployment with complete documentation.

Interview Findings Summary

Theme 1: Information Fragmentation and Research Fatigue

Participants consistently expressed frustration with the fragmented nature of salary research, describing a process of "hunting across multiple websites" and "never feeling confident about the numbers." P1 noted that JobStreet provides ranges but "doesn't explain if those numbers include EPF or are just basic salary," while P3 mentioned spending hours cross-referencing Glassdoor, LinkedIn, and asking friends, only to receive conflicting information. The lack of Malaysian-specific data was a recurring pain point, with P4 stating, "Most of the detailed information I find is for Singapore or the US, and I have no idea how to translate that to Malaysia." Participants desired an integrated platform that provides context-rich salary data specific to Malaysian market conditions, including breakdowns of what compensation packages typically include and how cost of living affects real purchasing power across different cities.

Theme 2: Negotiation Anxiety and Cultural Barriers

Every participant expressed some degree of anxiety about salary negotiation, with P2 describing it as "terrifying because you're worried they'll just withdraw the offer and give it to someone else." Cultural factors played a significant role, as P4 explained, "In Malaysia, we're taught to be grateful for what we're offered, not to push back or seem demanding." This cultural dimension manifested in specific language concerns, with P5 noting, "I don't know how to negotiate politely in a Malaysian context—if I'm too direct, I might offend them, but if I'm too soft, they won't take me seriously." Participants unanimously expressed strong interest in a private practice environment, with P3 stating, "If I could practice with an AI where nobody would judge me for saying the wrong thing, I would use that constantly before any real negotiation." The desire for feedback was particularly strong, with participants wanting to understand not just what to say, but how their approach might be perceived by Malaysian employers.

Theme 3: Employment Rights Knowledge Gaps and Vulnerability

While participants demonstrated awareness of basic rights like overtime pay, deeper questioning revealed significant knowledge gaps that left them vulnerable to exploitation. P2 recounted, "My first contract said I had to give three months' notice to resign, but they could terminate me with one week's notice. I didn't know if that was legal, so I just signed it." P1 admitted, "I've heard of the Employment Act, but I've never actually read it—it seems really long and complicated, and I don't know which parts apply to me." The contract review process was consistently superficial, with P4 explaining, "I skim through looking for the salary number and the start date, but honestly, I don't understand a lot of the legal language." When asked about automated contract review, participants showed enthusiasm tempered with concern about accuracy, with P5 stating, "I would definitely use it, but I'd want to know it's actually checking against real Malaysian law, not just general advice." Trust factors included citation of specific legal sections, explanations in plain language, and transparency about what the tool can and cannot determine.

Theme 4: Integration Value and Cost-of-Living Awareness

Participants consistently emphasized the value of integration over single-purpose tools, with P5 explaining, "The problem with having one site for salary data, another for practicing negotiations, and another for legal stuff is that I have to remember to use all of them and manually connect the dots." The cost-of-living consideration emerged as more important than initially anticipated, particularly for participants comparing opportunities in different cities. P3 shared, "I got offers from a company in KL at RM 5,000 and one in Penang at RM 4,200. Everyone said take the KL offer because it's higher, but I had no way to figure out if I'd actually have more money left over at the end of the month." P1 wished for "a calculator that shows me my actual take-home after EPF and tax, then subtracts realistic costs for rent and living expenses in different cities so I can see real numbers, not just gross salary." The integration of all features into a single workflow was seen as reducing friction and increasing likelihood of actually using the tools, with P4 noting, "If everything is in one place and I can go from checking salary to practicing negotiation to reviewing my contract without switching platforms, I'm much more likely to actually do all those things instead of just the first one and forgetting the rest."

Conclusion

The interview findings validate and refine several key design decisions for WageWise. The information fragmentation theme reinforces the importance of the integrated platform approach rather than building yet another single-purpose tool. The strong negotiation anxiety and cultural sensitivity requirements emphasize the need for the AI negotiation coach to specifically address Malaysian workplace norms and provide culturally-appropriate feedback, not just generic negotiation tactics. The employment rights knowledge gaps confirm that the chatbot should prioritize educational explanations with legal citations rather than assuming users have foundational knowledge, while the contract review feature must clearly cite specific Employment Act sections to build trust.

The interviews revealed specific language and feature requirements that quantitative surveys could not capture: the need for plain-language legal explanations, concerns about whether salary data includes EPF, desire for feedback on negotiation tone and cultural appropriateness, and preference for visual workflows that guide users through the integrated process of salary research, negotiation preparation, contract review, and location comparison. These insights will directly inform UI design, prompt engineering for the AI components, and documentation to ensure WageWise addresses real user pain points rather than assumed problems.

## Requirement Analysis

Requirements analysis focuses on organizing, validating, and modeling the gathered requirements based on questionnaire findings to ensure clarity and feasibility for the WageWise system. This section identifies key stakeholders, defines functional requirements organized by module with priorities informed by user feedback, and specifies non-functional requirements that govern system performance and user experience.

### Stakeholder analysis

The stakeholder analysis identifies key individuals and groups involved in or affected by the WageWise system, defining their roles, needs, and interactions with the platform based on empirical research findings.

Primary Stakeholder: Fresh Graduates and Final-Year Students

Role: End users who utilize WageWise to research salaries, practice negotiation, learn employment rights, and evaluate job offers across locations. They are the primary beneficiaries of the system and represent the target user base validated through questionnaire responses (100% of respondents were final-year students, 80% from IT/Computer Science fields).

Needs (validated by research): Accurate salary predictions for Malaysian job market (90% currently use platforms like JobStreet but lack comprehensive market intelligence), safe environment to practice negotiation without judgment (validated by 90% positive interest in AI negotiation coach), easy-to-understand explanations of employment rights (85% confident about overtime but only 60% about notice periods, indicating targeted educational needs), ability to compare job offers accounting for cost-of-living differences.

Interaction with System: Users access WageWise through the Streamlit web interface running on their local machine, input job details to receive salary predictions aligned with their expected salary ranges (RM 2,500-5,999 based on respondent distribution), engage in conversational practice with the negotiation coach to build confidence from current average of 3.45/5, ask questions to the employment rights chatbot focusing on commonly misunderstood provisions like notice periods and sick leave, and compare net income across Malaysian cities for informed location decisions.

Secondary Stakeholder: Early-Career Professionals (0-3 years experience)

Role: Users who have entered the workforce but continue to navigate salary negotiations and career transitions, particularly when requesting raises or changing employers.

Needs: Similar to primary stakeholders, with additional emphasis on negotiation scenarios for raises within current employment and guidance on evaluating external opportunities against current compensation. This group benefits from the system's ability to track market rate changes and practice navigating employer objections about budget constraints.

Interaction with System: Access negotiation coach for raise discussions using the 'Asking for a Raise' scenario, verify that employment contracts from new offers comply with Employment Act provisions, and use cost-of-living evaluator when considering relocation for new positions or promotions.

Supporting Stakeholder: Project Developer

Role: Responsible for system development, testing, deployment, and maintenance. Ensures technical feasibility and quality of all components while incorporating user feedback from research findings.

Needs: Clear requirements specification grounded in empirical data, access to quality training data (Malaysian job dataset), technical documentation for libraries and frameworks used, and feedback mechanisms to identify bugs and improvement opportunities post-deployment.

### Functional Requirements

  
Module 1: Salary Prediction and Intelligence

FR-1.1: The system shall allow users to input job details including job title, industry (with emphasis on IT/Computer Science), years of experience, education level, and preferred location to generate a personalised salary prediction.

FR-1.2: The system shall display a salary benchmark explorer that allows users to browse median salaries by job role and Malaysian city without requiring full prediction inputs, enabling lightweight exploration before committing to detailed entry.

FR-1.3: The system shall predict salary ranges showing the 25th, 50th (median), and 75th percentiles based on user inputs, covering the RM 2,500–5,999 range where 90% of respondents set their salary expectations.

FR-1.4: The system shall display a prediction reliability indicator for each result, shown as: (a) the number of records used from the training dataset that match the input criteria, and (b) a plain-language confidence label (e.g., "High confidence — based on 200+ records", "Low confidence — limited matching data") instead of statistical confidence intervals.

FR-1.5: The system shall display the dataset source name and its collection period alongside all salary figures, so users understand the basis and recency of the data presented.

FR-1.6: The system shall allow users to create and compare up to three salary scenarios side by side (e.g., varying job title, location, or experience level), displaying the predicted ranges for each scenario in a comparison table.

FR-1.7: The system shall provide comparisons of predicted salary against industry averages adjusted for different Malaysian cities, addressing user requests for location-aware salary intelligence.

FR-1.8: The system shall allow users to download a salary prediction report in PDF format containing the input parameters, predicted ranges, benchmark comparison, and data source details, for use during interviews and offer evaluations.

Module 2: AI-Powered Negotiation Coach 

FR-2.1: The system shall provide one to two fixed predefined negotiation scenarios (‘The Lowball Offer’ and ‘Asking for a Raise’) for users to practise salary negotiation, addressing the moderate negotiation confidence (3.45/5 average) identified among target users. The scope is intentionally limited to keep the feature lightweight and achievable within the project timeline.

FR-2.2: The system shall allow users to submit a negotiation response within a selected scenario and receive a simple AI-generated reply simulating an HR manager or supervisor, creating a low-pressure practice environment without requiring complex multi-turn dialogue management.

FR-2.3: Upon completing a scenario, the system shall display a short feedback summary (two to four bullet points) highlighting one strength in the user’s response and one key improvement suggestion, with tone guidance relevant to Malaysian workplace communication norms. The feedback is intended as a brief coaching prompt, not a comprehensive evaluation.

   
Module 3: Employment Rights Chatbot

FR-3.1: The system shall answer natural language questions about Malaysian employment laws including the Employment Act 1955 and Industrial Relations Act 1967, with emphasis on commonly misunderstood provisions.

FR-3.2: The system shall cite specific statutory sections (e.g., "Section 60A of Employment Act 1955") when providing legal information, enabling users to verify responses against the source legislation.

FR-3.3: The system shall provide a topic browser presenting predefined employment rights categories including overtime, annual and sick leave, termination, probation, salary deductions, and working hours allowing users to select a topic and receive a structured summary without needing to type a question.

FR-3.4: The system shall allow users to paste employment contract text excerpts for automated review, where the system identifies clauses that may conflict with Employment Act 1955 provisions and flags them with the relevant statutory reference.

 
Module 4: Cost-of-Living Evaluator

FR-4.1: The system shall calculate net take-home pay after EPF (11% employee), SOCSO, and income tax deductions based on the user’s gross salary input, translating the 70% user awareness of EPF/SOCSO into concrete purchasing power figures.

FR-4.2: The system shall allow users to compare job offers across different Malaysian cities (Kuala Lumpur, Penang, Johor Bahru, Kota Kinabalu, etc.), presenting side-by-side net income and affordability results to address user requests for location-aware career tools.

FR-4.3: The system shall display default city-level cost breakdowns by expense category (rent, groceries, transportation, utilities, healthcare) for each selected location, sourced from district-level cost-of-living indices, providing a transparent baseline for expense estimation.

FR-4.4: The system shall allow users to customise individual expense values within each category (e.g., adjusting the default rent estimate to reflect their actual or expected accommodation cost) rather than relying solely on city averages, recognising that lifestyle choices and personal circumstances vary significantly between users.

FR-4.5: The system shall calculate monthly disposable income after all expense deductions for each selected location, enabling direct comparison of real financial viability across cities and job offers.

FR-4.6: The system shall compare the user’s net salary against a living wage benchmark or minimum recommended cost level for the selected city, displaying whether the offered salary meets, falls below, or exceeds the threshold required for financial sustainability in that location. This aligns directly with the project’s core goal of evaluating wage adequacy beyond nominal salary figures.

FR-4.7: The system shall provide visualisations (pie charts, comparison cards, and affordability indicators) showing expense distributions, savings potential, and living wage comparisons across selected cities, supporting intuitive interpretation of cost-of-living data by non-finance-trained users.

### Non - Functional Requirements

NFR-1: Performance Requirements

NFR-1.1: Salary predictions shall be generated within 3 seconds of user input submission to maintain engagement and usability.

NFR-1.2: AI chatbot responses (negotiation coach and employment rights) shall be delivered within 5 seconds for typical queries, balancing thoroughness with responsiveness.

NFR-1.3: The salary prediction model shall achieve a minimum R² score of 0.80 on holdout test data, ensuring predictions are sufficiently accurate to build user confidence in market value understanding.

NFR-1.4: The system shall run smoothly on computers with minimum 8GB RAM and store models/data within 6GB disk space, ensuring accessibility for typical student laptops.

NFR-2: Usability Requirements

NFR-2.1: The user interface shall be intuitive, requiring no more than 5 minutes for new users to understand navigation and basic features, addressing the need for accessible tools for non-technical users.

NFR-2.2: All text shall be displayed in clear, readable fonts (Times New Roman or Arial, minimum 12pt) with sufficient contrast for extended reading during research and practice sessions.

NFR-2.3: Error messages shall be user-friendly, explaining what went wrong and suggesting corrective actions rather than technical jargon.

NFR-2.4: The interface shall be responsive, adapting layouts for different screen sizes (minimum 1280x720 resolution) to accommodate various laptop and desktop configurations.

## Software Architecture design

WageWise adopts a Three-Tier Client-Server architecture as its overall software architecture. This architecture separates the system into three distinct layers: the Presentation Tier (Flutter mobile frontend), the Application/Logic Tier (Python-based backend services and AI components), and the Data Tier (Supabase PostgreSQL database and vector stores). This separation of concerns is a fundamental principle of modern software engineering and is particularly well-suited to WageWise for three key reasons.

First, the Three-Tier architecture enables independent scalability: the AI inference services (salary prediction, RAG chatbot) can be scaled independently from the database or the mobile application without affecting other tiers. Second, it enhances maintainability, as changes to the machine learning model in the Application Tier do not require modifications to the Flutter frontend or the database schema. Third, it improves security by ensuring that the Data Tier is never directly accessible from the client device — all database interactions are mediated through authenticated API endpoints in the Application Tier.

### Architecture Overview

Figure 4.1 illustrates the Three-Tier architecture of WageWise. The Presentation Tier consists of the Flutter mobile application running on the user's Android device, exposing four module screens. The Application Tier comprises three core service components: the Salary Intelligence Service (Python, scikit-learn, XGBoost), the AI Chatbot Service (RAG pipeline with ChromaDB and LLM API), and the Cost-of-Living Evaluation Service (calculation engine with statutory deduction logic). The Data Tier consists of the Supabase backend providing PostgreSQL relational storage for user profiles, prediction history, and evaluation records, as well as a ChromaDB vector database storing embeddings of Malaysian employment legislation.

## Security Design

### System-Level Security 

Security in WageWise is addressed at multiple layers of the architecture. At the network level, all communication between the Flutter mobile client and the backend services is transmitted exclusively over HTTPS with TLS 1.3 encryption, ensuring that sensitive data such as salary information and personal details cannot be intercepted in transit. The Supabase backend enforces Row-Level Security (RLS) policies at the database level, ensuring that users can only read and write their own records and cannot access data belonging to other users even if they possess a valid JWT token. The Application Tier services are deployed behind API gateway endpoints that require Bearer token authentication for all requests; unauthenticated requests are rejected with HTTP 401.

### User Authentication and Authorization 

WageWise uses Supabase Auth for user authentication, which implements the OAuth 2.0 / OpenID Connect protocol with JWT-based session management. Upon successful login, Supabase Auth issues a short-lived access token (1-hour expiry) and a long-lived refresh token (7-day expiry) stored securely in the device's Android Keystore via the Flutter Secure Storage package. Access tokens are automatically refreshed using the refresh token without requiring re-authentication. Role-based authorization distinguishes between Standard User (all four modules, own history) and Admin (additional aggregate analytics access). All new registrations default to the Standard User role.

### Data Protection  

Sensitive user data is protected through a combination of encryption-at-rest and data minimisation practices. Supabase stores all database data with AES-256 encryption at rest. Passwords are never stored in plaintext; Supabase Auth uses bcrypt hashing with a work factor of 12. The application does not collect unnecessary personal information: only email, name, and app preferences are stored. When users delete their accounts, all associated records are permanently deleted from the Supabase database through a cascading delete operation. AI API calls to external LLM providers (Google Gemini/OpenAI) are made with ephemeral, session-scoped requests that do not retain user data beyond the duration of the API call, in compliance with provider data handling policies.
